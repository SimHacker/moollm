# Human Slop — drive-by AI accusations and stochastic parrots of human origin

> **The accusation performs the crime it alleges.** A drive-by "this is AI slop" comment is a
> thought-stopping phrase, heard elsewhere, repeated without understanding, contributing
> nothing — which is the definition of slop. Doctor, heal thyself. SQUAWK.

This is the companion to [no-ai-slop](SKILL.md). That skill fights slop generated by models.
This document is for the mirror failure: low-effort *human* slop, especially the two slogans
that recur in every AI discussion — "this is AI slop" and "stochastic parrot" — deployed as
substitutes for thought. Point here the next time it happens; it will happen next week.

## The by-definition argument

Credit where due — an HN commenter (horsawlarway, July 2026) stated the core problem exactly:
an LLM is a machine that generates content statistically similar to what it was trained on,
and it was trained on forums like this one. **The content here will look like AI-generated
content, and AI-generated content will look like the content here. By definition.** So the
daily ritual of accusing any and every article of being AI slop, backed by "my AI radar is
perfect" and some hokum about detecting style, is pseudo-intellectual witch-burning. There is
AI content everywhere, just as there was (and is) underwhelming marketing content everywhere —
and there are engaging, interesting articles, some of them written with AI.

**Vote on the content and move on with your lives.** Judge what's on the page. If it's wrong,
say what's wrong. If it's empty, say what's missing. Whether a human, a model, or both
produced it changes nothing about either critique.

## Perfect radar is how witch hunts work

The "I can always spot it" claim has a precise and ugly social analog: **"my transdar is
perfect."** The same certainty powers bigots who harass cis women — Michelle Obama among many —
up to and beyond peeking into bathroom stalls and violent confrontation, all in the name of
"protecting women" they never gave a damn about before, because the target doesn't conform to
their traditional ideal of femininity. Governments have built policy on that certainty — the
US and UK among them, in the company of the world's proudest authoritarian regimes.

The mechanics transfer exactly:

- The detector is confident and unfalsifiable ("I can always tell").
- The test is conformity to the accuser's aesthetic, not any property of the accused.
- The false positives land on innocent people — writers whose prose is too clean, too
  structured, insufficiently misspelled. (Observed in the wild: a commenter accused of
  posting AI because they *didn't misspell any words*.)
- The enforcement is the point. The accusation regime doesn't protect the community; it
  polices style and performs vigilance.

A witch hunt is not made virtuous by being about prose.

## "Stochastic parrot": a slogan masquerading as an explanation

The other squawk. People who deploy "LLMs are just stochastic parrots" as a drive-by argument
almost never know what the phrase is from, because they heard someone else say it and
understood it only at the shallow syntactic level — which is to say, they are parroting.
Stochastic parrots of human origin. SQUAWK SQUAWK.

**What the paper actually says.** Emily M. Bender, Timnit Gebru, Angelina McMillan-Major, and
Margaret Mitchell (as "Shmargaret Shmitchell"), *On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots: Can
Language Models Be Too Big?* (FAccT 2021) —
[the paper](https://s10251.pcdn.co/pdf/2021-bender-parrots.pdf). It is not a claim that LLMs
are literally parrots, and not primarily an argument about consciousness. It is a critique of
the *risks of ever-larger models*: bias inherited from undocumented web-scale training data,
environmental and financial costs, concentration of power, and the human tendency to
anthropomorphize fluent text and attribute understanding where there may be none. "Stochastic
parrot" was a cautionary metaphor about statistical generation. The phrase then escaped into
pop culture and became exactly what its authors were warning about in the other direction: a
fluent token sequence people repeat without understanding.

**The paper has critics too** — from the other flank. Michael Lissack's response, *The
Slodderwetenschap (Sloppy Science) of Stochastic Parrots*
([arXiv:2101.10098](https://arxiv.org/abs/2101.10098)), argued the paper was an advocacy piece
reciting harms without acknowledging presuppositions or weighing benefits. You don't have to
pick a side to notice the pattern: the debate among people who *read* the paper is about
methodology and trade-offs. The slogan among people who didn't is about nothing.

**Why the slogan fails as an argument.** It mistakes a mechanism for an explanation — the
pathology Lem spent a career dissecting (*Solaris*, *His Master's Voice*, *Golem XIV*): humans
mistaking their own conceptual categories for universal truths. "It's just predicting tokens"
has the same explanatory content as "your brain is just neurons firing" — by that reductionist
standard, the accuser doesn't understand anything either. And the metaphor insults parrots
upward: teach a parrot to squawk `main() { printf("Hello, world!\n"); }` and it cannot tell
you what the program outputs, fix a bug in it, or write a larger one on request. Whatever
LLMs are doing — compressed generalizable knowledge, emergent problem-solving, iterative
debugging — "parrot" does not describe it, and repeating the word does not analyze it.

## The price of admission

If you must accuse, do the work. Paste the suspect text into an LLM and have it produce a
*specific* critique of how and why it fails — if you can't write anything more interesting
than that, your comment wasn't worth posting. The bar for calling something slop is the same
bar no-ai-slop sets for the model: **name the sin, quote the evidence, or hold your squawk.**
A complaint that isn't even good enough to be a prompt is human slop — and if you can't clear
that bar, sit on your hands, however many fingers they have.

The claim-ledger discipline applies to accusations too: CONFIRM with evidence, DISPUTE with
specifics, or ASK. "I can tell" is none of these.
