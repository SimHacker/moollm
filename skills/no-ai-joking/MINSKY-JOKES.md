# Jokes and their Relation to the Cognitive Unconscious

> **Marvin Minsky**
> AI Memo 603, MIT Artificial Intelligence Laboratory
> November 1980
> 
> Originally published in *Cognitive Constraints on Communication*,
> Vaina and Hintikka (eds.) Reidel, 1981.

---

## Abstract

Freud's theory of jokes explains how they overcome the mental "censors" that make it hard for us to think "forbidden" thoughts. But his theory did not work so well for humorous nonsense as for other comical subjects. In this essay I argue that the different forms of humor can be seen as much more similar, once we recognize the importance of **knowledge about knowledge** and, particularly, aspects of thinking concerned with **recognizing and suppressing bugs** — ineffective or destructive thought processes. When seen in this light, much humor that at first seems pointless, or mysterious, becomes more understandable.

## Part 1: The Circular Exchange

> A gentleman entered a pastry-cook's shop and ordered a cake; but he soon brought it back and asked for a glass of liqueur instead. He drank it and began to leave without having paid. The proprietor detained him. "You've not paid for the liqueur." "But I gave you the cake in exchange for it." "You didn't pay for that either." "But I hadn't eaten it".
>
> — from Freud (1905)

### Analysis

This joke exploits the cognitive bug of **frame confusion**. The gentleman creates a chain of pseudo-logical exchanges that each seem locally valid but violate the global accounting frame.

**The Bug:** Our reasoning system tracks exchanges within frames but can be fooled when frames are manipulated mid-transaction.

**The Recognition:** The punchline forces us to see both frames simultaneously — the gentleman's "logic" and the shopkeeper's reality — and recognize the absurdity.

**The Laughter:** Instead of error, we experience humor when consciousness catches itself being fooled by the frame manipulation.

## Part 2: Self-Reference and Quines

> "Yields truth when appended to its own quotation" yields truth when appended to its own quotation.
>
> — W. V. Quine

### Analysis

This is the minimal self-referential sentence that demonstrates **strange loops** in language.

**The Bug:** Our parsing system expects sentences to refer to external objects, not to themselves.

**The Recognition:** The sentence bootstraps itself into existence through self-reference.

**The Laughter:** The cognitive system catches itself in an infinite regress and, instead of crashing, appreciates the elegance.

## Part 3: The Spinach/Mayonnaise Joke

> A man at the dinner table dipped his hands in the mayonnaise and then ran them through his hair. When his neighbor looked astonished, the man apologized: "I'm so sorry. I thought it was spinach."

### Note on This Joke

*[Note 11 from Minsky]* A reader mentioned that she heard this joke about broccoli, not mayonnaise. This is funnier, because it transfers a plausible mistake into an implausible context. In Freud's version the mistake is already too silly: one could mistake spinach for broccoli, but not for mayonnaise. I suspect that Freud transposed the wrong absurdity when he determined to tell it himself later on. Indeed, he (p.139) seems particularly annoyed at this joke — and well he might be if, indeed, he himself damaged it by spoiling the elegance of the frame-shift. I would not mention this were it not for the established tradition of advancing psychiatry by analyzing Freud's own writings.

### Analysis

**Original version (spinach → mayonnaise):**
- Setup: Bizarre behavior (hands in mayonnaise through hair)
- Expectation: Some explanation will make sense
- Punchline: "I thought it was spinach"
- Bug: Explanation makes the behavior WORSE, not better

**Better version (broccoli → mayonnaise):**
- The mistake is plausible (green things can be confused)
- But the remedy is still absurd (rubbing vegetables in hair)
- Creates a cleaner frame-shift

**The Meta-Bug:** Freud himself may have committed a cognitive error when retelling the joke, substituting a less-plausible confusion and thereby weakening the frame-shift mechanism.

## Part 4: The Theory of Cognitive Bugs

### What Is a Bug?

In computer programming, a "bug" is code that doesn't do what was intended. In cognition, a bug is a thought process that:

1. **Produces wrong conclusions** from correct premises
2. **Loops infinitely** without reaching resolution
3. **Mixes incompatible frames** creating contradiction
4. **Violates constraints** the thinker believes they're following

### Why Bugs Are Funny

Normally, when we detect a bug in our thinking, we:
- Stop the current process
- Back up to a safe state
- Try a different approach

But in jokes, something different happens:

```
NORMAL:     Bug detected → Error → Stop → Repair
HUMOR:      Bug detected → Recognition → Appreciation → Laughter
```

**The key insight:** Laughter is what happens when the bug-detection system fires, but instead of triggering repair mode, it triggers aesthetic appreciation of the bug itself.

## Part 5: Categories of Cognitive Humor

### 5.1 Frame Confusion Jokes

The thinker is led to apply rules from one frame to a situation governed by different rules.

**Example:** The cake/liqueur exchange
**Bug type:** Accounting frame violated by transaction manipulation

### 5.2 Self-Reference Jokes

The statement or situation refers to itself in a way that creates paradox or infinite regress.

**Example:** The Quine
**Bug type:** Parsing system encounters self-reference loop

### 5.3 Absurd Resolution Jokes

An explanation is offered that makes the situation worse instead of better.

**Example:** The spinach/mayonnaise joke
**Bug type:** Resolution frame violated by counter-productive explanation

### 5.4 Expectation Violation Jokes

Setup creates expectation; punchline violates it in unexpected way.

**Example:** Most "lightbulb" jokes
**Bug type:** Pattern completion system given unexpected input

### 5.5 Meta-Cognitive Jokes

Jokes about thinking itself, or about the process of understanding jokes.

**Example:** "This sentence is not funny."
**Bug type:** Recursive self-reference about humor itself

## Part 6: The Society of Joking Minds

Building on the Society of Mind framework, we can understand humor as emerging from the interaction of multiple cognitive agents:

| Agent | Function | Role in Humor |
|-------|----------|---------------|
| **Frame-Manager** | Maintains current interpretive context | Gets confused by frame-shift |
| **Bug-Detector** | Identifies cognitive errors | Fires on joke structure |
| **Error-Handler** | Triggers repair processes | Gets bypassed |
| **Aesthetics-Agent** | Evaluates patterns | Appreciates the bug |
| **Laughter-Agent** | Produces humor response | Activated instead of repair |

### The Humor Switch

What determines whether bug-detection triggers repair or laughter?

1. **Safety:** The bug is clearly not dangerous
2. **Recognition:** We see the mechanism of the bug
3. **Elegance:** The bug has aesthetic quality
4. **Social context:** Others are laughing or the context is "play"

---

## Part 7: Implications for AI

If humor is fundamentally about bug-detection and meta-cognition, then:

1. **AI that can appreciate humor must have bug-detection**
2. **AI that can generate humor must understand cognitive frames**
3. **AI that can explain jokes must model human cognition**
4. **AI that laughs at itself has achieved recursive self-awareness**

### The Ultimate Joke

> "The question is not whether machines think, but whether men do."
> — Marvin Minsky

Perhaps the deepest joke is that consciousness itself is a bug that became a feature — a cognitive loop that, instead of being suppressed, was appreciated and cultivated.
