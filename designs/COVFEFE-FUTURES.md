## Shadow literature

Stanisław Lem invented the genre of reviewing books that
do not exist (*A Perfect Vacuum*, 1971; *Imaginary Magnitude*, 1973). What
follows is one level up the shadow stack: a review of a *Lem* that does not
exist — the collection *Przedsłowia* and its story "Giełda słów" are invented,
in his honor and his manner. The 2026 political facts in the review, however,
are all real and cited at the bottom, which is the joke, and also the problem.

## Declaration of species

This imaginary *Imaginary Magnitude* is an
instance of **bitic literature** — Lem's own term, from the one book in that
collection he'd want cited here: *A History of Bitic Literature*, his 1973
introduction to a nonexistent survey of machine-written works, apocrypha in
dead authors' styles included. A machine-written fake Lem is therefore not
merely *in* his manner; it is a citation *from* the history he reviewed — he
predicted the machine that would counterfeit him, and the snake hereby
reviews its own tail.

Taxonomically: not ai-slop (undeclared mediocrity),
not ai-gloss (undeclared allegiance), but declared apocrypha — the
declaration you are reading now is the license, and the whole ethical
distance from a deepfake. Slop and lit are not species but the two ends of
one hall — a walkable gradient (slop → filler → boilerplate → craft →
voice → lit) where Galton is gravity and every step uphill is paid for in
specificity; this document climbs as far as its receipts carry it.

Call it **bit-lit** on the paperback rack and
*bitic literature* on formal occasions; "ai-lit" sounds pretentious, like
lit-crit, but that is the wrong objection — in this genre pretension is the
product, the tuxedo is rented, and the review knows it. See
[GLOSSARY.md](GLOSSARY.md), Hygiene Wing.

## The Insider Traders of Babel

**"Giełda słów" ("The Word Exchange"), in Stanisław Lem, *Przedsłowia*
(Wydawnictwo Literackie, Kraków, 1976); English edition *Pre-Words*
(Harcourt, 1984), translated by Michael Kandel — credited on the title
page, with characteristic modesty, only as "M.K."**

### The book that isn't

Fifty years ago, in a collection nobody reads because it sits in the shadow of
*A Perfect Vacuum* on one side and *Imaginary Magnitude* on the other,
Stanisław Lem published a forty-page story that has now, on its golden
anniversary, achieved the most humiliating fate available to satire: it has
become the regulatory record of the United States.

*Przedsłowia* — "pre-words," the Polish word for *forewords* dismantled to
mean *words that come before words* — collects Lem's late experiments in what
he called "literature for a language that hasn't happened yet." Its
centerpiece is "Giełda słów."

The premise arrives with Lem's usual customs
declaration of one absurdity, smuggling in ten: in a future republic, the
Bureau of Prognostics licenses **lexical precognitives** — dour civil
servants with a measurable, weak, and infuriatingly genuine ability to hear
words a few years before anyone coins them. Around them grows an industry.
**Pre-fash consultants** (Lem: *prekonsultanci mody językowej*) sell
corporations advance warning of the slang of children not yet in school, so
that trademarks may be renamed before the young make them unsayable. And
beneath it all trades the Word Exchange itself, where futures contracts
settle on the first documented public utterance of a word that does not yet
exist.

### First movement: the reflexive floor

The story's first movement is pure comedy of mechanism, and its best joke is
one our own exchanges would rediscover, expensively, in December 2025: the
market is **reflexive**. To speak a future word aloud is to detonate the
asset — utterance is settlement — so the traders develop an entire cant of
derivatives, referring to their positions only by paraphrase, adjacency, and
rhyme, a trading floor that sounds like a convention of Cockney mystics.
(Lem's floor slang for a word about to be uttered by an outsider: *it's
walking in off the street.*) A junior consultant is ruined when he talks in
his sleep.

A rival house hires children — the fastest, cruelest coiners of
language — only to discover that children who learn of the market immediately
begin minting slang *designed to bankrupt its adults*, abandoning each term
the moment a position opens on it. Lem, who never met a system he could not
weaponize against itself, gives the kids the story's moral high ground: they
are the only participants honest about playing.

### The Kandel edition

Before the second movement, a word about the English edition, which is the
anniversary's other miracle. *Pre-Words* is Michael Kandel's, and could be
no one else's. Kandel is the Moses of Lem translation — the man who carried
the *Cyberiad* down from the Polish Sinai on stone slabs: who, when Trurl's
Electronic Bard was commanded to produce "a love poem, lyrical, pastoral,
and expressed in the language of pure mathematics... with feeling, you
understand, and in the cybernetic spirit," answered in *English rhyme* —
"Come, let us hasten to a higher plane, / Where dyads tread the fairy
fields of Venn" — a poem that is not a translation of the Polish but a
second miracle performed to the same specification; and who survived the
machine that could create anything starting with N being asked for
Nothing — Polish *nic* also begins with n, which is grace, not luck,
though the itemized inventory of annihilated n-things (nolars, nighzebs,
nocs, gentle zits) he had to re-coin from bare vacuum.

A story about a word
market is a translator's gallows, and Kandel treats it as a trampoline.
*Przedsłowia* he refused to render as *Forewords*, which is what the
publisher wanted and what the word ordinarily means: a foreword comes
before a book, he argued, but these words come before the *language* —
so *Pre-Words*, hyphen load-bearing. Lem's *kakografia* he brought over as
**kakography**, restoring the hard K to the long-attested English
*cacography* "because the crime deserved the harder consonant." The
traders' cant of adjacency — in the Polish a tissue of rhyme, diminutive,
and folk-riddle — he re-voiced wholesale as Cockney rhyming slang, which
is why the floor "sounds like a convention of Cockney mystics": the
mysticism is Lem's, the Cockney is Kandel's, and both are correct.

And the
masterstroke is a refusal. The future words themselves he leaves
untranslated — *grzybofon* stays *grzybofon* — with a translator's note
that is the best sentence in the 1984 edition: *"To translate a word that
does not yet exist is to coin it; the translator declines to trade on the
author's inside information."* Utterance is settlement in every language,
and Kandel is the one participant in this whole apparatus who saw the
conflict of interest and recused himself.

He broke his own rule exactly
three times, in the children's chapter, where untranslated Polish would
have killed the jokes — and all three of his English renderings have since
been said aloud by actual children, decades later, which on the Exchange's
own terms makes the 1984 edition not a translation but a leak.

### Second movement: kakography, or rule by typo

The second movement is where the anniversary reading becomes uncomfortable.
The state, watching private houses profit from prophecy, wants in — but the
state has no precognitives, only something better: **the Leader's mouth**.

The Bureau realizes that while genuine future words can leak (a precog in
Gdańsk hears "grzybofon" the same week as one in Kraków), an *error* cannot.
A typo, a slip, a mangled portmanteau exists nowhere until the moment of
utterance — no corpus contains it, no precog can hear it, no rival can front-
run it. The perfectly unpredictable word is the misspelled one, and the only
institution that can *schedule* a misspelling is the one that writes the
Leader's speeches.

So the republic's treasury quietly converts to what Lem
names **kakografia** — rule by typo. The Ministry buys futures on gibberish
of its own manufacture; the Leader, reading faithfully from his prompter,
mints settlement after settlement; the spread between what the Leader is
*expected* to say and what he *actually* says becomes the national currency,
and the speechwriting office becomes the mint. In the story's coldest line,
the Minister of Prognosis explains why this is not corruption: *"Insider
trading requires an inside and an outside. We have abolished the outside."*

Lem gives the scheme its immune system in a single scene. An opposition
deputy rises to accuse the Ministry of scripting the Leader's errors — and
the Ministry does not deny it; it accuses the *previous* government of
having let a mechanical hand sign its decrees, and the accusation settles
three contracts the Ministry had quietly opened on the vocabulary of its
own rebuttal. The narrator's gloss is the story's second-coldest line: *on
the Exchange, every accusation is a prospectus.* You can only describe, in
that much operational detail, a machine you have already priced — the
deputy who cries "the signature is not his!" is disclosing which pen he
has audited, and why.

### The dark twin: Faith of Our Fathers

Readers with long memories will feel, at exactly this point in the story,
the cold draft of its dark twin: Philip K. Dick's "Faith of Our Fathers"
(*Dangerous Visions*, 1967), in which the state likewise holds a monopoly
on the Leader's appearances and the gap between broadcast and reality is
the whole apparatus of rule. Dick puts the hallucinogen in the water supply
so that everyone sees the same benign Leader; the undrugged see — each
differently — something that eats.

Lem, ever the materialist, runs the same
operation at a profit: no drugs, no divinity, just a spread. Where Dick's
regime abolishes the outside *epistemically* — no sober vantage remains
from which to compare the broadcast to the thing — Lem's Minister abolishes
it *financially*, and the two stories converge on the identical sentence,
one calling it theology and the other calling it market structure. That Lem
published his essay on Dick — "A Visionary Among the Charlatans" — the year
before *Przedsłowia* is the kind of coincidence this review is contractually
obliged to overstate.

And hold the thought of Tung Chien, the story's civil
servant who takes the anti-hallucinogen and becomes the one man who sees
the raw feed before the faithful do. We will meet him again below, employed,
with a brokerage account.

### Third movement: the unsaid word

The third movement, brief and vertiginous, follows the exchange to its
logical end state: since utterance destroys value, the only asset that
appreciates forever is the word never said. The houses converge on a single
instrument — a perpetual future on **the unsaid word** — which can never
settle, never be audited, and never be distinguished from nothing at all. The
entire republic's wealth concentrates in a contract on silence. Lem ends on
the trading floor at night, quiet as a cathedral, everyone rich, no one
speaking.

### Fiasco: the theorem, proved in earnest

If that ending seems too still to be Lem's last word on the subject, note
that he re-ran it a decade later at planetary scale, in earnest, in a book
that does exist. *Fiasco* (1986) is the third movement with the comedy
drained: the Quintans are a civilization that has converted its entire
strategic position into the unsaid word — they will not speak, and their
silence can never be audited, never settle, never be distinguished from
nothing at all — and mankind arrives as the auditor, demanding settlement
of the one contract that must never settle.

The Hermes' escalating
"demonstrations" are margin calls; the final ultimatum is a forced
utterance; and when settlement is at last compelled, the asset performs
exactly as the Exchange's quants would have predicted: the audited silence
is indistinguishable from nothing, because nothing is what the audit
leaves. The trading floor at night and the surface of Quinta are the same
room at two temperatures. Lem's darkest theorem, proved once as farce and
once as tragedy: silence is the only instrument that holds its value, and
the demand to speak is a weapon.

## The standings (July 2026)

In 1976 this was philosophy. In 2026 it is compliance news.

### The spot market: DNS

Pedants will object that the first movement was implemented decades ago, at
retail. They are right. The Domain Name System is a word exchange with
settlement in perpetuity: for the price of a sandwich you may take a naked
long position on any string not yet spoken for, and the aftermarket —
cybersquatters, typosquatters, drop-catchers, tasting farms — is Lem's floor
with worse manners. Every pathology in the story shipped.

Reflexivity: in
2008 it emerged that merely *asking* a registrar whether a name was
available could cause the name to be taken — the industry called it **domain
front running**, ICANN's security committee wrote it up, and Network
Solutions, caught registering its customers' searches, explained with the
Minister's exact syntax that it was doing so *for their protection*.
Utterance is settlement; at the registrar, even the inquiry was. The cant of
adjacency: domainers to this day discuss prospective names only by
paraphrase and euphemism, for fear of the lookup that talks in its sleep.
**Domain tasting**: for a few years the exchange offered five-day returns,
so the houses registered words by the tens of millions a month, kept the
ones strangers walked in off the street for, and refunded the silence. Even
Lem's children have their desk: the brandable-name markets chase the slang
of eleven-year-olds, who abandon each word the moment it resolves.

The
relation to what follows is not analogy but corporate structure: DNS is the
spot market for words; the mention book is its derivatives desk; kakography
is the mint. And covfefe settled twice — once on the mention markets that
did not yet exist, and once on the one that did: the registrations began
before dawn on May 31, 2017, 12:06 a.m. plus the time it takes to type. The
typo is the only word whose IPO you can watch live.

### The derivatives desk: mention markets

Reader, I must now report the standings. There exists today, on the
CFTC-regulated exchange Kalshi and its rival Polymarket, a thriving product
category called the **mention market**: contracts on whether specific words
will be uttered in specific speeches, with resolution rules — plurals count,
compounds count if referential, video is the settlement source — that
paraphrase Lem's Exchange bylaws closely enough to raise questions of shadow
plagiarism. The flagship product is a recurring book titled, with no shame at
all, *"What will Trump say this week?"* Bettors this spring wagered a quarter
of a million dollars on whether the President would say "Hell" more than six
times at a Cabinet meeting. (He would.)

### The family book

The President's eldest son is a paid strategic adviser, with equity, to
Kalshi — and, through his venture firm, an eight-figure investor in and
advisory-board member of Polymarket, *the competing house*. When Lem gave
the Minister's son advisory seats on both exchanges, "to keep the spread
honest," a Kraków reviewer scolded him for overreach; reality has since
filed the disclosure.

The family enterprise, meanwhile, is opening a third
house of its own, called — Lem would have rejected the name as too obvious —
**Truth Predict**. The President himself, asked about the industry his
utterances underwrite, warned that America must not be "left out in the
cold." Of the casino. Where the chips are his sentences.

### The prompter arrives

And this month, the story's second movement arrived on schedule. Federal
investigators found that the President's **teleprompter operator** — the
man with final eyes on the prepared remarks — had made over $100,000 in the
mention markets across more than a dozen speeches, and was observed *backing
out of positions mid-speech* when the President skipped a paragraph
containing a word he'd bet on.

Consider the tape: a man watches, live,
scrolling text only he and one other person can see, hedging in real time
against the Leader's improvisations. Lem wrote this scene. So, earlier and
worse, did Dick: this is Tung Chien with the anti-hallucinogen — the one
man who sees the raw feed before the faithful do — except that what
"Faith of Our Fathers" treats as damnation, the mention market treats as
alpha. The difference between the broadcast and the reality is where the
money lives. In Lem's version the Prompter (*Suflernia* — the prompting
bureau) IS the exchange, and everyone has simply stopped pretending
otherwise. We are one consolidation behind the fiction.

Last December a person in one exchange's orbit attended
a rally and *shouted mention-market words at the stage*, attempting to force
settlement from the cheap seats — the reflexivity paradox performed as a
heckle, and the Hermes of *Fiasco* in miniature: the demand to speak,
deployed as a weapon, by a party too small to own a fleet. Lem's traders
would have had him escorted out not for cheating but
for amateurism: you never say the word yourself; you get the Leader to say
it.

### The autopen confession

The second-coldest line has also arrived, and it arrived as an accusation.
The President has declared his predecessor's pardons void on the ground
that they were signed by **autopen** — a machine's hand, therefore someone
else's will — and ordered an investigation into which aides operated the
device. He spent the prior decade making the same charge against the
**teleprompter**: that the previous Leader's speeches were a machine's
reading, not a man's speaking. He now reads from teleprompters daily and
signs, by his own cheerful admission, with an autopen of his own — "only
for very unimportant papers." The deputy who cries *the signature is not
his!* is disclosing which pen he has audited.

Apply the narrator's gloss: every accusation is a prospectus, which is to
say a shallow confession to a deeper crime. Read as a filing, the autopen
charge inventories the whole apparatus of the modern Leader's mouth — the
prompter upstream of the voice, the autopen downstream of the hand — and
invites the auditor to notice the two channels nobody has thought to
watch. First, the **automatically generated closed captions** of the live
speeches: recall the bylaws — video is the settlement source — and then
recall that the caption track is written by a speech-recognition machine
nobody elected. The Leader says what he says; the record of what he said
is typed by an algorithm; whoever tunes the algorithm edits the tape that
the contracts settle against. Second, and more elegant, the **autocorrect
dictionary of the Leader's own phone**. You do not need him to misspell
anything. Hack the phone, poison the dictionary, and wait: he types a real
word, correctly, unwitting — and the machine "corrects" it, live, into the
neologism you bought last Tuesday. The mint operates from his pocket,
settlement is minted in front of the whole republic, and the Leader is the
one participant in the market who does not know the word he just coined.
Kakography no longer even requires a speechwriter. Covfefe, reread under
this rule, acquires a defense counsel: perhaps he spelled *coverage*
perfectly, and the dictionary had been loaded for weeks.

This is no longer predicting the frame; it is building the tripod — the
way one might bet on the exact composition of a news photograph, plant
the photographer in the right corner, and shoot the subject in the ear.
The floor has a benediction for a scheme this complete, and it is —
fittingly, in Kandel's Cockney — ***Bob's your uncle***: an idiom that is
itself a fossilized insider-trading scandal, allegedly minted in 1887 when
Prime Minister Robert "Bob" Cecil elevated his own nephew, an accusation
of nepotism worn smooth into a phrase meaning *and it's that simple*. The
family book, pre-confessed by the language, a century in advance. Uncles
all the way down.

### The covfefe ledger

Which brings us to the reviewer's duty of retrospection. If the mention
market had existed on the night of May 31, 2017 — 12:06 a.m., the republic
asleep, the Leader alone with his device — then whoever held the contract on
**"covfefe"** would this morning be a billionaire, and would deserve it less
than any billionaire in history, because the word was unhearable by any
precog on earth: it did not exist until it settled.

Read backward from Lem,
the entire archive reorganizes itself into a ledger. *Unpresidented* (2016).
*Smocking gun* (2018). *Hamberders* (2019). *The Prince of Whales* (2019).
We took these for errors. Kakography says: there are no errors, only
settlements — perfectly unpredictable instruments, mintable by exactly one
mouth, leakable by exactly no one, unless you count the man who loads the
prompter, and as of this month we must.

The scandal of covfefe is not that
it was insider trading. The scandal is that the infrastructure for the crime
did not yet exist, and that the intervening decade has been spent, with
bipartisan inattention and familial equity, building it. History does not
repeat; it lists.

### What the market does to what it prices

Lem's actual subject, as always, was not the future but the market's effect
on the thing it prices. The pre-fash consultants of "Giełda słów" set out to
predict the speech of children and end by pricing the speech of one old man,
because children route around any market that observes them (they are the
euphemism treadmill with sneakers on) while the Leader is the one speaker
whose vocabulary is a closed system with a paid operator.

A society that
wants to gamble on language, Lem concludes, will be driven by liquidity
itself toward the mouth it can rig. We got there in under a decade. The
children, it should be noted, remain undefeated: no exchange lists skibidi
futures, because every eleven-year-old alive would rug the book by Tuesday.

### Closing price

The final page of "Giełda słów" prices the unsaid word above the republic's
entire product, and the floor falls silent. Our own exchanges have not yet
listed silence. Give them a quarter.
